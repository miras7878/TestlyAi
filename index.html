What Drove the Improvement?
AI causal attribution analysis
Based on user session recordings and scroll heatmaps, the AI attributes the conversion lift to three factors:

1. Social proof block — Users who saw the review count (n=412 reviews) had a 2.3x higher conversion rate than those who scrolled past it.
2. CTA button color shift (blue → teal) — Eye-tracking data suggests a 40% longer fixation time on the new button.
3. Headline reframe — "Start your free trial" outperformed "Get started" by reducing perceived commitment.
⚠️
Potential Risks & Considerations
Before you ship, review these flags
1. Novelty effect: A portion of the uplift may be attributed to the novelty of the new design. The AI estimates this at ~8–12% of the observed lift. Consider running for 2 more weeks to validate.

2. Segment heterogeneity: Mobile users show a smaller lift (+18%) vs desktop (+52%). Ensure the redesign is well-optimized for mobile screens before full rollout.

3. Seasonal timing: This test ran over a holiday period. Validate results against a non-holiday baseline segment.

Process

How A/B Testing Works

Four clear stages from idea to decision.

01
💡

Form a Hypothesis

Identify what you want to improve (e.g., conversion rate), propose a change, and define a measurable success metric.

02
🔀

Split Your Traffic

Randomly assign visitors to control (A) and variant (B) groups. Random assignment is key to eliminating selection bias.

03
📏

Collect Data

Run the experiment long enough to gather a statistically meaningful sample. Stopping too early leads to false positives.

04
✅

Analyze & Decide

Use statistical significance (p-value < 0.05) to determine if results are real. Then ship the winner or iterate.

Key Concepts

Statistical Foundations

Statistical Significance

A result is "statistically significant" when it's unlikely to have occurred by random chance. The industry standard threshold is 95% confidence (p < 0.05).

Sample Size

Larger samples produce more reliable results. Use a sample size calculator before starting to ensure your test has enough power to detect meaningful differences.

Minimum Detectable Effect

The smallest improvement you want to be able to detect. Setting this helps calculate required sample size. Typically set between 5–20%.

Type I vs Type II Error

Type I (false positive) declares a winner when there is none. Type II (false negative) misses a real improvement. Both are managed through confidence and power settings.

Novelty Effect

A temporary boost from the change being "new." Mitigate by running tests for full business cycles (at least 2 weeks) and comparing returning vs new users.

Simpson's Paradox

A trend that appears in grouped data can disappear or reverse when the data is combined. Always segment results by key dimensions to avoid misleading conclusions.
